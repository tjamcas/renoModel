Exterior

Although structural improvements are being made on the older part of the house, one should not assume that the original construction and subsequent renovations were somehow inferior in craftsmanship or poorly designed. It is important to recognize that the Cheboygan house was built and subsequently renovated for different uses, under different circumstances, and with limited resources over a period of time that saw large and rapid advancements in technology and infrastructure.

When Great-great Grandpa Frost originally built the house, access to Northern Michigan for winter vacation visitors was very limited. At the time of the cabin's original construction, I-75 and the US interstate highway system did not exist. And so, the cabin was only built for use from late spring, summer, and early fall use.

Grandpa and Grandma Castle built additions and made renovations that transformed the cabin into a four-season home. They made these improvements while occupying the house. This greatly complicated the timing, scale, and cost of the construction that could be reasonably managed while maintaining a livable space.

North exterior of house in October

A new doorwall and window combination has been installed in the kitchen that will open the indoors to the outdoors. The new, combination kitchen window is similar in layout to the three-section picture window on the original cabin's front wall that looked out and onto Lake Huron and Bois Blanc Island. The new windows will allow plenty of Northern summer light to enter the kitchen along with fresh breezes and sounds from the Straits of Mackinac.

North exterior of house in January

There was 1-2 inches of snow on the ground with patches of grass here and there. The amount of snow on the ground is significantly less than what Grandpa and Grandma Castle experienced in the years after they relocated from Detroit to Cheboygan in 1981.

After some delays from the supplier, the order for the replacement vinyl siding shipped, and the sections beneath the kitchen window were installed. Vinyl fixture boxes were also received and the new exterior lamp fixtures will be installed in the coming week.

Kitchen

Despite the many measures that Grandma and Grandpa Castle took to insulate the house from the cold Northern winters, they could not have predicted recent climate trends of less snow and more extreme temperature fluctuations in the Cheboygan area that have made the house more susceptible to pipe-freezing and damage.

Although Great-great Grandpa's Frost's original cabin was built for limited, warm weather use, this year's renovations will return the house to being a true year-round living space. The plumbing will be better protected through a combination of re-routing lines into interior walls and building new framewalls with blown-in insulation.

In addition to further winterizing the house, this year's renovations will open up an airy space that connects the kitchen, dining and living rooms.

New entry doorwall

Along with the new doorwall and combination window, a new framewall was constructed in the kitchen interior. The original house wall can be seen behind the framewall - it is constructed of upright pine timbers. It served as both the exterior siding and as the frame of the house on which the interior pine v-groove paneling was fastened. Originally, house wires to outlets and switches were exposed on the cabin walls prior to the remodels and renovations that were undertaken in the 1970's and 80's. The new 2x4" framewall in the kitchen has been built alongside the original house wall timbers. This new framewall provides a larger, structured space to run plumbing and electrical inside the wall as well as to spray foam insulation.

Plumbing trench

A jackhammer has been used to remove the bathroom slab floor and to make a trench between the kitchen and the bathroom. New plumbing will be routed beneath the replaced slab with drain lines laid with sufficient pitch to the house septic line.

Drywalled and painted kitchen

Having been drywalled and painted, the kitchen is ready to receive cabinets, flooring and trim. Foam insulation has been blown in behind the walls. Plumbing has been laid in the trench and covered in concrete. A layer of leveler will be appplied to the slab and allowed to cure prior to the installation of the flooring. Between the slab and the finished flooring planks, a vapor barrier and pad will be laid. The flooring "snaps" together and will float on top of the slab and padding. The padding provides insulation and also dampens the sound of footsteps on the hard flooring.

Kitchen floor and trim

The kitchen and dining room recessed lights have been installed but the electrical wiring has not been competed, and the lighting in this photo is dim. Even unlit, you can see how the alder cabinetry and trim and the white oak flooring in the natural light have come together to create a very warm space in the kitchen. The flooring is an engineered wood plank with a white oak veneer. It is manufactured in Kentucky from Appalachian oak.

The flooring will require some attention and care because it is a natural wood product, but it shouldn't be fussy or overhelming. This is a more sustainable choice than some of the vinyl-based flooring products that are currently popular. This same flooring will be extended into the dining room and living room in the 2021/2022 fall-winter phase.

Appliance wall cabinets

Cabinets arrived on February 4, and countertops were installed on March 18. You may notice that the partial wall between the dining room and kitchen has been removed. In its place is a knotty alder trim box to contain the fridge. The appliances will go in their approximate, previous locations. With the old entry door in the corner removed, counter space around the appliances has been lengthened and now extends from the refrigerator to the doorwall exterior wall.

Sink wall cabinets

Solid wood Mouser cabinets were selected for the kitchen. The frame is constructed of plywood, with knotty alder exteriors in natural stain. Due to warm weather humidity, plywood cabinetry is preferred over cabinets constructed with MDF or particle board. The appearance of these alder cabinets will deepen into a richer and more amber hue as they age over the years.

Kitchen sink

Like the old kitchen, the new kitchen has a stainless-steel, double basin sink. The old kitchen had a 50-50 configuration. The new kitchen is a 1 - 3/4 configuration. The 3/4 basin will be fitted with a disposal. The two bored holes in the counter will be used for the pull-down faucet and the disposal push-button.

Cabinet and countertop materials

This photo provides a close-up of the countertop, cabinet, and cabinet hardware material. The countertops are made of engineered quartz. Engineered quartz is non-porous so that it is easy to clean, stain-resistant (but not stain-proof), heat-resistant (but not heat-proof), and scratch resistant (but not scratch-proof). So, while it is durable and should hold up to lots of use, common-sense still applies without being obsessively fussy. Fussiness, fortunately, is something to which our family is not disposed.

Interior

In addition to the major renovations being undertaken in the kitchen and front bathroom, the dining room will also be modestly refreshened in this year's project. The wainscotting and existing drywall has been removed and replaced with new drywall. The original v-groove, pine paneling was uncovered. In the kitchen, all of the original paneling was found to be scarred and cracked and generally unusable. However, in the dining room, a fair amount of the pine paneling was in good condition. It is hoped that this material can be re-incorporated into future phases. There won't be a sufficient amount to panel a room or a wall, but it could possibly see use as trim or shelving.

Other interior improvements were made with an eye to the future. Plumbing was replaced and re-routed to lessen cold-weather exposure and potential freezing and pipe bursts. Electrical circuits were wired to a new subpanel in the utility room. An array of recessed can lights will extend across the dining room and kitchen ceiling.

The dining room carpet has not yet been replaced. In the 2021-2022 project, the living room floor will get an added subfloor layer so that the living room and dining room are at a matching continuous level. At that time, a new floor will be laid in the dining and living room. When the kitchen flooring material was procured this past November, an additional amount to cover the square footage in the living and dining rooms was also acquired. This was done in order to ensure a match between the three rooms, to guarantee availability of product, and to lock in on a lower line-foot rate while material costs are sharply rising during the pandemic. The planks are currently being held in storage with Petoskey Flooring.

Painted dining room

The jackhammered trench between the kitchen and the bathroom is now filled with concrete. New plumbing is routed beneath the replaced slab with drain lines laid with sufficient pitch to the house septic line.

The new plumbing replaces hot and cold feed lines that were previously routed along the exterior walls of the house and were susceptible to winter freezing. In recent years, the lack of snow exposed the house to "Artic Clippers" where frigid temperatures combined with strong winds. This combination penetrated its way through house crevices and froze plumbing. The new buried lines will be much better protected.

An added benefit of the new plumbing connection between the kitchen drain and the house septic system, is that a disposal can be installed. Disposal of food scraps in the kitchen sink will create a healthier and more efficient microbial environment in the house septic system.

Bathroom and bedroom entrance

The pocket door entrance into the bathroom has been drywalled and painted. The finish trimming can be seen in another photo on this page. The bathroom entrance has been shifted to the right in order to fit a 28" door. The sliding door will be a simple, Shaker styled, 2-panel door. As rooms are finished in the coming years, the existing slab doors will be replaced with similarly styled, 2-panel doors.

Utility room electrical sub-panel

A new electrical sub-panel, that connects to the main breaker panel in the "baby's" bedroom, has been installed in the utility room. As rooms are renovated, their electrical circuits will be routed to the utility room panel. Eventually, the breaker panel in the "baby's" bedroom will no longer be required and can be removed.

Moving to a new electrical panel serves three purposes. First, it moves the breaker panel out of a living space and the room gains some wall space. Second, the newer panel is modernized to higher amperage service. Third, this co-locates the plumbing and electrical service in the garage, wellroom and utility room. In the future, when technological advancements make it feasible and affordable, we can consider solar paneling connected to battery banks in the colocated service area.

Master bedroom exposed plumbing

Between the front remodeled bathroom and the well room in the garage, plumbing has been routed through or on interior walls. Here, the exposed plumbing has been routed along the master bedroom interior wall and tucked up at ceiling level.

The visible plumbing is unsightly but it is only temporary. For now, the plumbing will remain exposed until the back bedroom and craftsroom/office are remodeled in a future phase. At that time, the plumbing will be concealed in either the wall frame or in a purpose-built soffit.

Bath

The front bathroom sits on the cement slab of the original cabin. Great-great Grandpa Frost's cabin consisted of five rooms and a sleeping porch. The five rooms were the kitchen, two bedrooms, one bathroom, and the dining room.

Originally, there was an exterior window in the bathroom. A large claw-footed, cast iron tub used to sit by the window. The tub access was not very friendly for young children or older adults. The tub was removed and the window was covered up when the bathroom received its first remodeling in the 1970's. The frame for the window opening can be seen in the photos.

The original window will remain covered and the free-standing tub will not return. The sink, toilet, and tub placement will not substantively change from the previous placement configuration. However, the tub and vanity will be replaced. The new tub is cast iron with easy step-in for young and old alike, and the surrounding tilework will have a simple, classic black and white scheme.

Bathroom wall demolition

This is a photo of the front bathroom demolition. This photo shows that the bathroom interior wall to the hallway has been removed. It will be replaced with a new wall with a pocket door. The bathroom door opening will be re-positioned so that a 28" solid-core, sliding door can be installed. The sliding door will create more room around the bath and allow privacy.

Bathroom slab floor removal

Similar to the kitchen the front bathroom was stripped down to the exterior walls. The original exterior walls are the upright timbers behind the new 2x4" frame walls. The cement slab was also removed to allow new interior drainlines to be installed between the kitchen and the bathroom and the house septic line.

Bathroom drywall hung

This closeup photo of the bathroom interior shows the tub stall and, to the right, the adjacent cubby after it was drywalled. The cubby is adjacent to the vanity as well as the tub. It will hold shelves that match the vanity and can be used by visitors to hold their toiletries.

In the top left of the photo, a soffit extends above the shower head and along this interior wall. It contains the hot and cold water plumbing. All water feeds within the bathroom, as well as between the pump/furnace room and the bathroom, has been re-routed. Where possible, plex tube plumbing was fed through interior walls. Where plumbing could not be fed inside the wall 2x4" framing, soffits were built to hold the plex tubing. Moving the plumbing away from exterior walls will prevent pipes from freezing during cold weather so that the house may remain open during winter.

Bathroom floor basket weave tiles

The black and white bathroom floor was laid in a vintage basket weave tile pattern. This smaller, tighter pattern masks imperfections in areas of older construction where walls bow or don't meet at perfect 90-degree angles.

The basket weave's geometric pattern in high-contrast, black and white color tricks the eye, so that to the casual observer, the border lines between tile pieces appear to bend. However, this is an optical illusion - upon closer inspection, it becomes more apparent that all tile lines are straight and meet at 90-degree angles.

Burns, gouges, and chips are another story. A simple hanging mirror will be selected for above the vanity. A wall light fixture is ready to be installed above the mirror.
